The Downloaders: Norwegians are financing the sexual abuse of children

They are the end users in an industry that uses children as sexual commodities. They pay to download images and videos showing children being sexually abused. They think they are invisible. But they are wrong. We have spent the past year travelling around Norway contacting them.

Chapter 1: "I smashed my computer last night."

When he hears what we know about him, his eyes well up with tears. The middle-aged man dressed in a shirt and sailing jumper leans back in his office chair and glances at the two doors to his office.

"I see."

The dog by his feet lazily wags its tail. On the narrow table separating us lie transcripts of images and lists of files containing sexualised images of young children. According to VG's material, these are files this man has downloaded.

He folds his arms tightly across his chest.

"I don't know what to say. You're a journalist. I have no idea how this will look when you're done with me. If this gets out, my life is over. How on earth did you find out all this about me?"

For ten months VG has been mapping and identifying Norwegians who are believed to have download images and videos showing child sexual abuse. The man sitting across the table from us is one of them.

The material we are now publishing has been uncovered by VG's Einar Otto Stangvik, one of Norway's leading data analysts. Two years ago he exposed a Conservative Party politician who had hacked his way into and stolen private images young girls had taken of themselves.

VG holds detailed information on 78 Norwegians who, according to our data, have spent time, money and resources on increasing the demand for videos showing children being abused.

We know who they are, where they live – and what was downloaded to user names, computers and IP addresses that can be traced back to them.

The data material in our possession is highly comprehensive. Apart from the police, few others have as full an overview of individuals believed to have actively downloaded child sexual abuse images in Norway.

The material consists of a total of 36 million lines of data, which is equivalent to a novel consisting of one million pages.

Exposing downloaders of this type of abuse is an extremely demanding task.

Some downloaders hide behind anonymous user names, secret e-mail accounts and multiple IP addresses.

But we found them all the same.

They are all men.

VG Helg has contacted ten of them.

We have travelled throughout Norway with hidden microphones and cameras and met them in parks, at petrol stations, in their homes and at their workplaces.

Two of them had taken a few drinks to give them Dutch courage before meeting us. One lost several kilos in weight and was unable to sleep after being confronted with what we knew about him. Two have asked for help to stop.

Seven of the ten admit to having done what our data suggests: downloaded images and videos showing sexual abuse of children. Three deny everything.

The downloaders are the end users in an industry that uses children as sexual commodities. The distributors behind the abuse can earn millions of kroner – some of which allegedly came from the men we have identified.

And they may be hiding an even darker secret: according to the police and experts, between 30 and 80 per cent of people who download child sexual abuse videos have themselves committed acts of child sexual abuse.

If our data is correct, VG knows the identity of up to 60 sex offenders.

The chance of any of these men getting caught by the police is minimal. The NCIS (National Criminal Investigation Service) is inundated with new cases.

"We've seen a dramatic increase in the number of cases in the space of only a few years. The amount of material confiscated in each case is enormous, often millions of images," says Lena Reif, head of the NCIS Sexual Crimes Unit.

Her unit is responsible for investigating downloading and sexual abuse of children. The NCIS continually monitors activities on several file-sharing networks. One day in August last year, the police registered 98 unique Norwegian IP addresses that downloaded or shared child sexual abuse files. In the course of two weeks, the number rose to 1,055 Norwegian IP addresses – and that was only from four file-sharing networks.

WHERE THE MEN LIVE

The men we found live all around the country. We have identified 78 of them, but VG Helg's material contains many more: we have information on a total of 373 Norwegian IP addresses to which sexual abuse files were downloaded.

The number of downloaders is increasing. But the resources needed to track down the downloaders are not being increased at the same rate.

"In 2007 and 2008, considerable resources were allocated to deal with internet-related child sexual abuse. The NCIS was allocated earmarked funds. Since then, resources in this areas have not been strengthened," says Reif.

Perhaps this is the reason why most of the downloaders VG has identified have been able to continue downloading without being caught by the police ­– and for a long time. A health care worker has been downloading undisturbed for more than ten years. According to him, he has seen all there is to see of child sexual abuse material.

The children in the videos that are downloaded are damaged for life, both physically and psychologically. An American report from 2010 showed that child sexual abuse increased the risk of anxiety, depression, eating disorders, post-traumatic stress and suicide attempts.

"Sexual abuse of children can lead to prolonged and lifelong health problems and increase the risk of suicide attempts and early death," says Siri Thoresen, a senior research at the Norwegian Centre for Violence and Traumatic Stress Studies. Thoresen studies the long-term effects of violence and sexual abuse.

There are myriads of websites where videos and images like these can be shared. Some of the websites log users and downloads and post them openly online. It was in these logs, containing details of over 36 million downloads, that VG found the Norwegian downloaders.

"Websites that offer child sexual abuse files do it for the money. The downloaders must pay. And when they do, they have to register. This means we can identify them in our logs," says VG's data analyst Einar Otto Stangvik.

"We don't know exactly what each individual has done; we haven't had a camera fixed on them while they downloaded. The only thing we actually know is that a computer at an IP address in the proximity of the man's home has logged on to the file-sharing site with a user name and an e-mail address that suggests that he is the one who did it," says Stangvik.

Nonetheless, the information we have has been enough to get several of the ten men VG contacted to admit to having downloaded videos and images of child sexual abuse.

The way in which the videos are stored on the internet is so devious that it takes insight, determination and money to find them. This is what the logs show:

To reach the website where the child sexual abuse files are located, you first have to click on a link on another website. This link takes you to the website where you can download the videos and images.

In other words: the men read about the films, click on a link that takes them to the files, and then download them.

Several of the file names leave no doubt about their content: '5 yo cum in mouth'. 'Baby 6 yo fuck anal'. Others are more cryptic but no less disturbing to read when you know what they contain: 'With my favourite pink teddy bear'; 'Bathtime'.

Other file names are completely anonymous, but websites with file descriptions give more details. For example, that file 'arkivez7.rar' contains 16 films of a mother sexually abusing her three-year-old daughter and 21 films and series of images of extreme child sexual abuse of an American ten-year-old.

The content of another file is described as '10-year-old girls plays with cock'.

Is it that acceptable to download child sexual abuse files as this man claims? The experts VG has talked to take a different view. Irishman Joe Sullivan is one of Europe's foremost experts in risk assessment of sexual predators:

"At some point during the procedural or judicial process, eight out of ten who are convicted once for possession of child sexual abuse material will admit that they have also committed acts of sexual abuse on children. That is my experience from treatment and research," says Sullivan to VG.

Sullivan is a forensic psychologist and runs a company called Mentor Forensics. In the past 20 years he has worked on risk assessment for organisations such as the National Crime Agency, the UK equivalent of Norway's National Criminal Investigation Service (NCIS).

"The question is not whether or not the downloaders will commit abuse; it is whether they already have, and in what context," he says.

When he is asked to find out whether someone who has been convicted for downloading is likely to commit abuse when released, he interviews them using a lie detector.

"I do that to confirm that what they tell me is true and not just something they think I want to hear. It's during these interviews that eight out of ten downloaders admit to having committed child sexual abuse before they were charged for possession of child sexual abuse images."

"Are there any good reasons for downloading child sexual abuse material?"

"None. I'll bet that the men you meet will give you a range of excuses. But the reality is that possessing these films and images shows that they have a sexual interest in children. That means that they represent a risk. The authorities must look at how much contact they've had with children over the years, because there is a strong likelihood that they've already committed abuse," says Sullivan.

Lena Reif, head of the Sexual Crimes Unit in the NCIS, also believes there is a connection between downloading material showing child sexual abuse and actually committing it.

"Thirty per cent of the individuals we know have downloaded child sexual abuse material have also committed child sexual abuse. It seems as if many download child sexual abuse images to normalise their own fantasies about abuse like this," says Reif.

This is the reason why the NCIS take downloading seriously, says police superintendent Bjørn-Erik Ludvigsen.

"Downloading files and viewing this type of abuse is, by extension, sexual exploitation of children," he says.

Ludvigsen is one of Norway's most experienced investigators in internet-related child sexual abuse material.

"We try to maintain a professional focus, in the same way forensic scientists do when dealing with damaged bodies on a daily basis. We can't show empathy towards all the victims we meet every day.; if we did, we’d make ourselves ill."

He is sitting in front of two monitors in the basement of the NCIS headquarters in Oslo. The room is called 'Ragnarok', named after the apocalypse in Norse mythology. This is where the investigators conduct their investigations into internet-related child sexual abuse material.

"It's good to have our own room for this part of our work, where we have to witness children's pain and fear. I won't deny that it's difficult to recruit police officers to this field of investigation."

Kaja Hegg in Save the Children has been working on online abuse of children since 2006. She considers it a problem that so many can download child sexual abuse material without worrying about getting caught.

"The risk of getting caught needs to be raised. The internet must not be allowed to be a free zone, It's unforgivable if the police don't give these cases priority. These are serious acts. It's about time that the Ministry of Justice be confronted with this, because it's their responsibility."

She is frustrated at the authorities' commitment to combat child abuse.

"Almost ten years ago, the Ministry of Justice appointed a working group for the prevention of internet-related child sexual abuse. After it submitted a good report in 2007, several measures were followed up, but since then all efforts have just tapered off," says Hegg.

"According to the Government's action plan, the efforts to combat the distribution of child sexual abuse images was supposed to be strengthened. What happened?"

"The efforts haven't been strengthened. There's no political will to do anything, and there's no system of organising expertise. The NCIS does a lot of good work, but if we're going to step up our efforts, that needs to happen in the police and the support systems. And that hasn't been done so far," says Hegg.

She is not the only one to react: exactly two years ago, in September 2013, lawyer Thea Totland wrote a letter alerting the Director of Public Prosecutions to the critical situation in the police force.

She wrote: "The treatment provided to children who are subjected to violence and sexual abuse is unworthy of a country governed by the rule of law." She went on to tell of how every week she encountered instances where the Norwegian police failed to take seriously the Director of Public Prosecution's instructions to give priority to violence and sexual offences committed against children.

"The Director of Public Prosecutions thought I was dramatising the situation. I can't see that any positive steps have been taken since then. The NCIS has improved its expertise over the years, but they lack the resources. The percentage of child sexual abuse cases that are dropped is close to 80 per cent," she says.

The the same year as Totland alerted the Director of Public Prosecutions, the Ministry of Justice signed the Global Alliance against Child Sexual Abuse, committing Norway to enhancing its efforts to investigate child sexual abuse.

However, this commitment has not led to more resources for the investigators in the NCIS.

"The NCIS has not received any extra resources to undertake the measures stated in the alliance agreement. And since then the problem has grown dramatically," says Lena Reif.

THIS IS HOW THEY SHARE FILES AND PAY FOR THEM

Many think that the files are shared on the deep web. They are wrong. Although the file descriptions are stored on the deep web, the actual files are often stored on the open web. The reason for this is simple: the individuals sharing the files want to make money from it.

WEBSITES WITH FILE DESCRIPTIONS

The downloaders hear about or make their own way to websites containing links to sexual abuse files stored on forums that are often hidden. These forums contain descriptions of the files and passwords if the files are password-protected.

STORED ON THE OPEN WEB

They follow the link from the hidden forum to a file-sharing website on the open web. This may take them via one or more advertising services where they have to view several advertisements before they can proceed.

LOGGING IN

They reach the file-sharing website, where they are often required to log in before downloading the sexual abuse files.

PREMIUM ACCESS

They often have to pay for premium access in order to download. Payments are made via HiPay, PayPal or similar payment services.

DOWNLOADING

Once they register and pay, they gain access to the file. They download it and unpack it. Some files are password-protected, in which case they have to use the password provided on the hidden forum to unpack them.

Cash flow

The market has multiple direct and indirect players.

Our focus is on the downloaders. They visit one or more websites on their way to the sexual abuse material. They enter via a forum, follow a link to an advertising service and proceed to a file-sharing website. During these three steps, banner ads and similar advertising is shown, generating traffic which in turn generates cash flows.

Once they reach the file-sharing website, they often pay money to complete the download.

A proportion of the money paid to the file-sharing website is often distributed to the individuals who uploaded the files. Sometimes uploaders demand payment in cash for the files, other times they acquire files through barter. Some files contain material they have produced themselves or have acquired free of charge, though not before investing a considerable amount of effort.

There are often several middlemen between the downloaders and the producers of the material.

Some producers are financially motivated, others are motivated more by fame, bartering or sexual preferences. In recent years we have seen several examples of filmed sexual abuse to order, particularly in countries like the Philippines and Thailand and other poor regions in Southeast Asia. These may involve the perpetrators' own children, street children or random victims.

The downloaders have contributed to sustaining an industry by showing their interest and by supplying it with fresh capital.

How we found the downloaders

It was VG's data analyst Einar Otto Stangvik who uncovered and analysed the data logs that provided us with the identities of Norwegians believed to have downloaded child sexual abuse material. It has been a demanding task, but it shows that downloaders are not as invisible as they thought they were. This is what Stangvik did:

36 MILLION DOWNLOADS

While working on another story, VG Helg became aware of the existence of websites all over the world which we suspected contained sexual abuse material. On further investigation it became clear that a certain type of file-sharing service was often used to distribute these files. We also discovered that many of these sites stored logs showing who had downloaded which files. On some sites these logs were openly accessible, and we began systematically downloading them.

VG Helg is now in possession of 36 million lines of data containing details such as time of download, file name, linking sites, IP address, user name, and e-mail address.

THE CHILD SEXUAL ABUSE FILES

Not all the 36 million downloads contain child sexual abuse material. To find out which files showed sexual abuse of children, we used the information obtained from both the open and the deep web to find the names of the files with this content. We gradually accumulated a small group of file names, which we then used to find even more. Our theory was that those who downloaded the files we knew about would also download other files.

THE NORWEGIAN USERS

Starting off with the 36 million downloads and the files we knew contained child sexual abuse material, we developed a system which gradually identified related files and then excluded files which we were fairly sure did not contain child sexual abuse material.

The first step in the search system identified who had downloaded the first files we knew contained videos showing sexual abuse of children

.

In the next step the search system filtered out the downloads that were not made from IP addresses in Norway or that originated in known proxy services etc. This left us with 550 Norwegian downloads of the initial files.

EXPANDED THE SEARCH

For each of the 550 downloads we had information on the IP address, file name, download time, the downloader's e-mail address and user name, and which websites they had come from (so-called referrers). We then proceeded to:

  • Retrieve every download that was made to the same IP addresses as each of the 550 files.
  • Retrieve every download that was made at the same time as each of the 550 files were downloaded.
  • Retrieve every download that was made to the same user name.
  • Retrieve every download that was made to the same e-mail address.
  • Retrieve every download that was made by users who had come from the same referrer as each of the 550 files.

This process resulted in a large number of downloads which we then sorted by limiting our search to downloads made from Norway. Several other filters were also activated to ensure that the downloads we obtained contained child sexual abuse material.

We then ended up with around 6,000 downloads of child sexual abuse material made from Norway. Several of the link sites to the downloads were also checked, and several files were assessed by the National Criminal Investigation Service (NCIS) to verify or dispel our suspicions regarding their content. This manual task required more filters, which were incorporated during the process. Once these were in place, we ended up with around 5,500 downloads made from Norway.

Identity

This analysis provided us with a list of user names and e-mail addresses that originated from Norwegian IP addresses. We sorted these according to number of downloads and then proceeded to investigate these individuals one by one, starting with those who had made the most downloads.

We tried to identify each individual using user names, e-mail addresses and other details registered in social media and search systems. We also managed to track down some of them via Skype and Twitter, and many via Facebook. In most cases, and particularly in the case of the individuals we chose to contact, we could verify connections between the user names, e-mail addresses and IP addresses in the logs and their actual identity using more than method.

What do we really know?

VG must take into account that the downloads may have been made by someone other than the men we have identified. The only thing we strictly do know is that a computer on an IP address where the man lives, have used a user name associated with this man's e-mail address to log on one or more file sharing sites - and then download abusive images and sexualized images of children. We have had contact with ten men we have identified. Seven of them admit that our information matches what they have actually done.

All the men VG confronted give explanations similar to the youth leader: they are not sexually aroused by watching children being sexually abused. Even though they watch child sexual abuse videos, they could never commit sexual abuse themselves.

Sexologist Margrete Wiede Aasland has many years of experience in treating sexual predators. She is not convinced by the explanations the men gave to VG.

"If it doesn't turn them on, they why do the download it? Sexual predators are known for making excuses and playing down what they do. Perhaps they believe it themselves. Many need to live under some kind of illusion in order to continue downloading."

Like Joe Sullivan, the Irish expert on child sexual abuse, she also sees a connection between downloading and committing physical abuse. She nods affirmatively when she hears Sullivan's finding that eight out of ten downloaders also commit physical abuse of children.

"That does not surprise me. It's not uncommon for people undergoing treatment here to admit more and more."

Her view is that as a crime, downloading sexual abuse videos is as serious as perpetrating abuse themselves.

"Even though they do not commit physical abuse offences, downloaders are party to sustaining an industry. The money they pay makes it lucrative to commit new abuses," says Aasland.

Money is what enabled VG to find the downloaders.

When downloaders want to talk to each other, they meet on secret forums in the deep web, but they share their images and films on the open web. They do this not to make it easier for each other, but to earn money from like-minded people. If you want to haul in really big money, you need to be where people can pay for the abuse with bank cards.

"And to do that, they have to register. This means we can identify them in our logs," says VG's data analyst Einar Otto Stangvik.

Before being allowed to download files, most websites require the downloaders to pay a subscription. If they download over several years – as many of them have – the amounts can quickly add up. One downloader who continues to download has previously been convicted for having paid NOK 100,000 for sexual abuse videos. Based on this information and the data on the number of users, teasers, and subscription fees, it is easy to see that these websites are raking in millions of kroner annually.

A considerable portion of this money also goes to the individuals who load the files up on the websites. Each time a file is downloaded by others, the uploader receives a small fee.

A file that is download thousands of times can generate sizeable revenues.

In a small town on the east coast of the United States, there is one mother who knows exactly how many times the films showing her daughter being abused have been downloaded. Because she is notified every time the police find the films of her daughter after new raids. The first time VG talks to her, she had received 12,521 notifications. By the time we talk to her half a year later, she has received 2,702 new notifications.

In other words: every single day she is notified of 13 persons who have downloaded the films showing her daughter being abused. And this is just the tip of the iceberg, because these are only the downloaders the police have manage to catch.

"Today I've receive six notifications. One came from Arizona five minutes ago, and another one from Montana half an hour ago. They caught five men there. All of them had images of my daughter on their computers," the woman tells us by telephone from the US.

Her daughter was sexually abused by her father as an infant. He was caught when he wanted to lend her to someone else on a secret forum for paedophiles. He turned out to be an FBI agent.

The mother sobs quietly when she talks about the day the FBI asked her to identify her daughter from recordings her husband had made.

"It was like identifying a dead person who was still alive. She's still breathing, but he killed her innocence. I know they showed me a video, but I just try to forget it. I threw up when I saw the pictures."

Our daughter dreamed of becoming a model, but her mother told her it was out of the question.

"I won't let her do it. She needs to find a regular job. How can we make sure that no one digs up that shit from her past and publishes it?"

According to the mother, the father was sentenced and sent to jail, but he gets out in a few years time.

"My daughter, on the other hand, has been given a life sentence, mentally and physically."

She may be right: the images of her daughter are among the most popular with downloaders.

Who are the people behind these websites distributing sexual abuse films? They live all over the world. The owners of websites VG has log data on live in the UK, Belgium, Andorra, the Ukraine, Russia, India, Vietnam, Japan, the Philippines, the US and Canada.

It does not take long for a reply: "I fully agree. I love rape."

Why we used these methods

Dear reader This story is based on extraordinary journalistic methods. It is extremely rare for VG to use hidden audio and video recordings when we meet interview objects, but in these instances it was the only way to document that Norwegian family men, health care worker, and well-educated men in business and industry admit to having downloaded extreme and in some cases violent sexual abuse material. Through the use of authentic recordings we have also been able to document how these men explain their actions.

In this connection we refer to clause 3.10 of the Ethical Code of Practice, which permits the use of hidden cameras and microphones when this "is the only possible way to uncover cases of essential importance to society." We consider the fact that Norwegian men are helping to finance sexual abuse that damages and destroys children's lives by their downloading to be a crystal-clear case of such importance.

The men we contacted were selected on the basis of the amount and type of sexual abuse material they have downloaded. Our investigations into some of them were abandoned when circumstances made it difficult to contact them.

We wanted to talk to the men about the information in our possession without their families or others close to them finding out, so as to protect the privacy and anonymity of both the men and their families.

We also wanted to be present to observe the men's reactions when there were confronted with the documentation VG had obtained. We therefore provided few details about the content of the story in telephone conversations during which meetings were arranged.

We adopted no hidden agenda or identity in the course of our research for this story. The men knew that they were meeting journalists from VG and that the findings would be published in VG.
